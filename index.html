Safety & Scams

General Vietnam
▾

Violent crime against tourists is virtually unheard of. Real risks are petty.

  • Bag snatching — #1 incident. Walk on the building side of the pavement, not the curb. Keep phone in a pocket, not in hand. Never hang a bag from a motorbike basket.
  • Taxis — use Grab (fixed fare, tracked) or stick to Vinasun/Mai Linh. Avoid unbranded cabs.
  • Currency — 20,000 VND and 500,000 VND notes look similar. Count before handing over cash.
  • Cyclo/xe ôm — agree fare before you get in. Drivers sometimes inflate after the ride.
  • Tour agents — book online or through your hotel. Street agents in HCMC and Hanoi routinely overcharge.
  • SIM cards — buy from official carrier booths at the airport only.
  • Dengue — no vaccine. Use repellent at sunrise and sunset, especially in the Mekong Delta.
Ho Chi Minh City
▾

Higher scam density than elsewhere. Stay alert in District 1.

  • Bag snatching from pedestrians is the primary risk — stay off the curb
  • Avoid cyclo riders who approach you on the street
  • Be cautious if a new acquaintance invites you to a bar — surprise bills are a known scam
Mekong Delta
▾

Very low crime risk in rural areas. Practical concerns only.

  • Dengue risk is highest here — repellent is essential
  • Carry water; heat and dehydration are the main hazard
  • Road safety — traffic rules loosely observed on rural roads
Hanoi
▾

Petty crime only. Old Quarter and Hoan Kiem Lake are highest-density scam areas.

  • Arrange airport pickup in advance — taxi scams at Noi Bai Airport are the most reported incident
  • Use Grab or Be for all city transport
  • Counterfeit goods are everywhere in the Old Quarter — assume anything suspiciously cheap is fake
Pù Luông
▾

Very low risk. No specific scam profile.

  • Limited healthcare nearby — carry a basic first-aid kit
  • Paths slippery after rain — stick to guided routes
  • Don't leave electronics visible in village homestays
Taipei
▾

One of the safest cities in the world. Minor risks only.

  • Taxi overcharging at the airport — use the meter or book via app
  • Fake charity/monk solicitations near temples — ignore and walk on
  • Main hazard is traffic — scooter crossings catch visitors off guard
Singapore
▾

Extremely safe. Standard urban awareness is sufficient.

  • Check change from taxis — some drivers return Malaysian Ringgit instead of SGD
  • Electronics shops at Sim Lim Square have a history of bait-and-switch — agree full price before buying
  • Bump-and-distract pickpocketing in crowded malls — check pockets if someone bumps you

Taipei

Feb 15–16NT$ (TWD) · 1 CAD ≈ NT$22.70Airport: TPE (Taoyuan)
About
▾

Taiwan is a small island of 25 million people off the southeast coast of mainland China, shaped by successive waves of indigenous, Han Chinese, colonial, and Japanese rule before becoming the seat of the Republic of China government in 1949. Taipei is its economic, political, and cultural centre: a cosmopolitan metropolis where incense-veiled temples blend seamlessly with neon modern street life.

The metro area holds around 4.5 million people, known for its world-class MRT, legendary night markets, and vast array of restaurants. Food is central to Taiwanese culture, costs are low, and ease of navigation frequently surprises first-time visitors.

Mid-February is a good time to arrive — Lunar New Year will have just passed and the island settles back into its everyday rhythm.

About
▾

Ho Chi Minh City — still widely called Saigon — is Vietnam's largest city and its economic engine, with a population of over 9 million. Its history runs from a Khmer fishing village to a French colonial stronghold to the focal point of the Vietnam War, renamed after the fall of Saigon in 1975.

For travellers, it's a city of relentless energy — chaotic motorbike traffic, excellent coffee, war history you can walk through, and neighbourhood life that rewards anyone willing to get off the main drag.

Washrooms
▾

More variable than elsewhere on this trip. In cities, cafés and restaurants are your most reliable option — step in and ask. Public toilets exist near markets and tourist sites and usually charge 2,000–5,000 VND; keep small change handy. Attended paid toilets are generally cleaner than free public ones.

In rural areas — including the Mekong Delta cycling route and Pù Luông — facilities are sparse. Use whatever's available before you leave a stop: your accommodation, a restaurant, a ferry terminal. Don't wait.

Two rules that apply everywhere in Vietnam: carry your own toilet paper (rarely provided), and don't flush it — plumbing can't handle it. Use the bin beside the toilet.

Fish Sauce (Nước Mắm)
▾

Fermented anchovies and salt, aged 12–15 months in wooden barrels. The fundamental condiment of Vietnamese cooking — used raw in dipping sauces (nước chấm), cooked into braises, and drizzled over almost everything. Quality varies enormously by grade.

Grades:

* 30°N — everyday cooking grade

* 40°N — premium; best for dipping where quality is most obvious

* 43°N — top-end, longer shelf life, more complex

* 60°N — rare first-press; unlikely outside Phu Quoc

Tasting: Compare the dipping sauce (nước chấm) at different restaurants — ratios and quality vary noticeably and reflect the cook's hand. Good fish sauce smells savoury and almost caramel-like, not aggressively fishy.

Factory tours: Phu Quoc island (not on itinerary) has 100+ factories open to visitors. Best is Khai Hoan — free English tours, no booking needed. Worth considering if Phu Quoc ever gets added as a side trip.

Where to buy in HCMC: Bến Thành Market East Gate (Phan Boi Chau St) — packaged goods section, multiple brands side by side. Look for Red Boat or Phú Quốc brand, 40°N+.

To take home: Sealed bottles, checked luggage only. Red Boat 40°N is also available in Canada but significantly cheaper here and fresher stock.

Cashews (Hạt Điều)
▾

Vietnam is one of the world’s largest cashew producers, primarily from Binh Phuoc province near HCMC. Quality at source is noticeably better than anything exported. Look for whole kernels (not broken), ivory to light gold colour, fresh smell. W180 or W240 on packaging indicates large, high-grade kernels.

Varieties to try:

• Salt-roasted whole — the baseline, shows quality clearly

• Salt and kaffir lime leaf — a southern Vietnamese twist, excellent

• Coconut-roasted — common in the Mekong region

Where to buy in HCMC: Bến Thành Market East Gate alongside fish sauce, but tourist-priced — negotiate. Better value at local dry goods shops in the same area. Avoid pre-packaged tins in tourist shops — usually stale.

To take home: Vacuum-sealed packs travel well in checked or carry-on luggage. Far cheaper than Canada and noticeably fresher.

Dried Shrimp (Tôm Khô)
▾

Small shrimp sun-dried to an intense, concentrated umami — one of the most useful pantry ingredients in Vietnamese and broader Southeast Asian cooking. Used in fried rice, congee, soups, salad dressings, and as a topping. A small handful transforms a dish. Mekong Delta produces some of the best in Vietnam.

What to look for: Bright orange-pink colour, firm texture, clean sea smell. Avoid anything grey, powdery, or with ammonia smell. Sold by weight at markets — buy loose rather than pre-packaged where possible.

Where to buy in HCMC: Bến Thành Market, Bình Tây Market (Cholon — larger, more local, better prices).

To take home: Sealed bag, checked luggage. Lasts months in a cool dry place, longer refrigerated.

Durian Products (Sầu Riêng)
▾

Off-season for a Feb–Mar visit — fresh Vietnamese durian runs May–September, peaking in June. Not worth seeking out. Best tried in Singapore instead.

Fresh durian — the famously pungent, custard-textured fruit — is not transportable home but worth trying in HCMC or the Mekong Delta where it’s grown locally. February is slightly off-peak but available. Monthong variety is the mildest entry point; Musang King more intense.

What to try fresh: Buy pre-opened from a market stall rather than whole — vendors will crack it and let you smell before committing. Eaten with sticky rice in some southern preparations.

Packaged versions for travel:

• Dried durian chips — concentrated flavour, addictive once you’re converted

• Durian candy and wafers — milder, good introduction

• Freeze-dried durian — closest to fresh texture, best quality option

Where to buy in HCMC: Bến Thành Market or any street fruit stall for fresh. Packaged versions at Ben Thanh or Saigon Square dry goods shops.

To take home: Packaged only, carry-on fine if sealed well. Declare at Canadian customs (dried fruit product).

Mekong Delta

Feb 19–25Cash only in rural areas
About
▾

The Mekong is one of the great rivers of Asia, rising in the Tibetan Plateau and flowing nearly 4,500 km through China, Myanmar, Laos, Thailand, and Cambodia before reaching Vietnam. Its delta — where the river fans out into a vast network of channels, islands, and floodplains before emptying into the South China Sea — is one of the most fertile regions on earth, producing more than half of Vietnam's rice and the bulk of its fruit, fish, and vegetables.

Can Tho is the largest city in the Mekong Delta and its de facto capital. The food identity is strongly rooted in Southern Vietnamese riverside cuisine — fresher and sweeter than the north, built around abundant local seafood, vegetables, and rice.

The cycling route runs from the outskirts of HCMC through Ben Tre (Vietnam's coconut capital), Tra Vinh, and into Can Tho. No ATMs between stops — stock up on VND in HCMC before departing Feb 18.

Washrooms
▾

Facilities are sparse in rural areas. Use whatever's available before you leave a stop: your accommodation, a restaurant, a ferry terminal. Don't wait. Carry your own toilet paper everywhere. Do not flush — use the bin beside the toilet.

Lunch on the Road
▾

Forget convenience stores — they're a city phenomenon in Vietnam and won't appear on rural Delta roads. What you'll find instead is better: a constant trickle of roadside stalls, market towns, and small family restaurants serving fresh, cheap food to order.

Stop when you see people eating. A busy stall with plastic stools and steam coming from a pot is always a good sign. No need to read a menu — point at what someone else is having.

Use market towns and ferry crossings as planned stops — gaps between settlements on back roads can be longer than expected.

What to look for:

Bánh mì — the easiest grab-and-go option. A vendor with a cart or basket, usually 15,000–30,000 VND. Portable, filling, no sit-down required.

Hủ tiếu — the Delta's signature noodle soup. Light, clear broth with pork or shrimp. Fast to serve, deeply local, and cheap.

Cơm bình dân — workers' rice shops. Point at pre-cooked dishes in pots: grilled fish, stir-fried vegetables, rice. Usually 30,000–50,000 VND for a full plate.

Fresh fruit — sold cut and bagged roadside for almost nothing. The Delta grows Vietnam's best: star apple, pomelo, citrus, coconut. Excellent mid-ride fuel.

Nước mía (sugarcane juice) — pressed on the spot, cold, about 10,000–15,000 VND. Hard to pass up in the heat.

Ca phe dua (coconut coffee)
▾

Best version on the entire trip. Floating market vendors at Cai Rang serve it with coconuts harvested that morning — rich, tropical, sweet. Order it wherever you stop along the river.

Coconut Candy (Kẹo Dừa)
▾

A Bến Tre specialty — the province you'll be cycling through. Made from coconut milk and malt syrup, slow-cooked and cut into small wrapped chews. Flavoured versions include pandan, durian, and ginger. Ubiquitous in the delta but Bến Tre is the source and quality is noticeably better here than in city tourist shops.

What to look for: Handmade versions from small producers over factory-sealed bags. Soft and yielding, not hard or waxy. Durian and pandan varieties are the most interesting.

Tasting / making: Some homestays and small workshops along the cycling route demonstrate production — Mr. Biker Saigon may include a stop. Worth asking the operator in advance.

Where to buy: Bến Tre markets or roadside producers along the cycling route. Also available in Cần Thơ's Bình Tây-style wet markets if you miss it on the road.

To take home: Individually wrapped, travels well in carry-on or checked. Lasts several weeks at room temperature.

To take home: Sealed bag, checked luggage. Lasts months stored cool and dry.

Tamarind (Me)
▾

A sour, sticky paste made from the tamarind pod — used in southern Vietnamese cooking for soups, braises, dipping sauces, and candy. The Mekong Delta grows it locally. Sharply sour with a slight sweetness; essential in canh chua (sour fish soup), one of the delta's signature dishes.

What to try: Canh chua is the best introduction — a sour tamarind broth with river fish, pineapple, tomato, and bean sprouts. Widely available at any local restaurant in the delta.

What to buy: Block tamarind (seedless paste) rather than the processed concentrate — more versatile for cooking at home. Also look for tamarind candy (kẹo me) as a snack — sweet-sour, sold loose at markets.

Where to buy in Cần Thơ: Cần Thơ Central Market. Buy block paste by weight.

To take home: Block paste wrapped tightly, checked luggage. Keeps for months. Candy is carry-on fine.

Shrimp Paste (Mắm Tôm)
▾

A thick, pungent purple-grey paste of fermented shrimp — more intense than fish sauce and fundamental to northern Vietnamese cooking. Used as a dipping sauce base (thinned with lime juice, sugar, and chilli), in soups, and with dishes like bún đậu mắm tôm (fried tofu and noodles). An acquired taste for most visitors but worth trying — it's the flavour backbone of Hanoi street food in a way fish sauce isn't.

What to try: Order bún đậu mắm tôm at a local Hanoi spot — the paste arrives on the side so you control how much you use. Good introduction without committing to a full mắm tôm-forward dish.

What to look for when buying: Smooth, even purple-grey colour. Should smell intensely savoury and fermented, not sour or off. Sold by weight at markets or in sealed jars.

Where to buy in Hanoi: Đồng Xuân Market — the city's main wholesale market, best selection and prices. Ask for jarred rather than loose if taking home.

To take home: Jarred and sealed, checked luggage only. Declare at Canadian customs. Strong smell even sealed — double-bag it.

Vietnamese Cinnamon (Quế)
▾

A different species from Ceylon or Cassia cinnamon — stronger, more complex, with a subtle spicy heat alongside the sweetness. Used in phở broth, braised dishes, and five-spice. Central Vietnam grows the best (quế Trà My from Quảng Nam province) but Hanoi markets are the practical place to buy, with good stock from northern and central producers.

What to look for: Thick, rough bark with a reddish-brown interior. Snap a small piece and smell — should be intensely aromatic, almost spicy. Avoid thin, smooth sticks which are likely lower-grade Cassia.

Where to buy in Hanoi: Đồng Xuân Market spice section. Buy whole bark rather than ground — stays fresh far longer.

To take home: Whole bark in a sealed bag, carry-on or checked. Lasts a year or more stored dry.

Star Anise (Hồi)
▾

Eight-pointed dried seed pod with an intense anise-liquorice flavour. Grown in Lang Son province near the Chinese border — one of the closest major growing regions to any city on your itinerary. Essential in phở broth, five-spice, and braised dishes. The difference between fresh Vietnamese star anise and the stale imported version in Canadian supermarkets is significant.

What to look for: Whole intact pods, dark reddish-brown, intensely fragrant when you snap one open. Avoid broken or pale pods — sign of age.

Where to buy in Hanoi: Đồng Xuân Market spice section alongside cinnamon. Buy whole, not ground.

To take home: Sealed bag, carry-on or checked. Lasts 1–2 years stored dry and away from light.

Black Pepper (Tiêu)
▾

Vietnam is the world's largest black pepper exporter. Phu Quoc and Binh Phuoc grow the most prized varieties — not directly on your route, but Hanoi markets stock northern and central Vietnamese peppercorns worth buying. Fresher and more aromatic than anything available in Canada.

What to look for: Whole peppercorns, uniform dark colour, heavy for their size. Crush one between your fingers — should release immediate sharp, floral heat. Pre-ground is not worth buying.

Where to buy in Hanoi: Đồng Xuân Market. Buy whole peppercorns by weight rather than pre-packaged.

To take home: Sealed bag, carry-on or checked. Keeps 2–3 years whole, much less once ground.

About
▾

Pù Luông Nature Reserve is the recommended trekking destination near Hanoi — flat valley paths, Black Thai and Muong villages, quiet, with extras including bamboo rafting and waterwheels. 165 km from Hanoi.

February is dry season — ideal conditions for trekking. Mist in the mornings lifts to clear skies by mid-morning.
Weather
▾

Cool, dry. February is dry season — ideal trekking conditions.

Washrooms
▾

Facilities are sparse in rural areas. Use what's available at homestays and rest stops. Carry toilet paper.

Leeches
▾

Leeches are the most likely nuisance. They're harmless but startling. Peak season is the rainy months (May–October); February is significantly better. Humid, shaded jungle sections at elevation can stay wet year-round, so don't rule them out entirely. Tuck pants into socks. Standard insect repellent doesn't reliably deter leeches — salt works better. Check yourself after every forest section.

Mosquitoes
▾

Mosquitoes are present year-round but reduced in the dry season. Dengue is the main day-to-day risk — spread by Aedes mosquitoes that bite during daylight hours, especially dawn and late afternoon. No treatment exists, so prevention matters: 20%+ DEET repellent, long sleeves and pants, and a bed net if sleeping somewhere without screens. Japanese Encephalitis (JE) vaccination is worth discussing with a travel medicine clinic before departure.

Ticks
▾

Ticks are present in jungle undergrowth. Do a full body check after any off-trail walking. Snakes are a low-probability but real hazard in forested areas. Stick to trails, don't reach into undergrowth, watch where you step. Your Pù Luông guide will know the terrain.

Rabies
▾

Rabies is present in Vietnam. Don't pet stray dogs or feed monkeys near temples.

Pack: DEET repellent, toilet paper, a small supply of tissues, and trekking poles if you have them — useful on slippery rice terraces.

Kaya (Kaya)
▾

A rich, fragrant jam made from coconut milk, eggs, and sugar, flavoured with pandan leaf and set with slow cooking. Spread thickly on toast with cold butter — kaya toast is Singapore's defining breakfast. Two styles: Hainanese (darker, more caramelised, uses more egg) and Nonya (greener, more pandan-forward, lighter). Both worth trying.

What to try: Kaya toast with soft-boiled eggs and kopi (local coffee) at Tiong Bahru Market or Toast Box. This is the breakfast to have your first morning in Singapore.

Where to buy: Tiong Bahru Bakery stocks a version. Ya Kun Kaya Toast sells their own jarred kaya — widely available and the most recognisable brand. Cold Storage supermarket has a good range for comparison.

To take home: Jarred, refrigerate after opening. Checked luggage — glass jars. Lasts several weeks refrigerated.

Gula Melaka (Palm Sugar)
▾

Dark, aromatic sugar made from the sap of coconut palm flowers — used across Southeast Asian cooking and baking. Tastes like a complex cross between brown sugar, molasses, and caramel with a faint smokiness. Sold in cylinders or discs. Completely different character from refined sugar and irreplaceable in dishes like cendol, ondeh ondeh, and coconut-based desserts.

What to try: Order cendol — a shaved ice dessert with pandan jelly, coconut milk, and gula melaka syrup poured over. Simple but shows the sugar's character clearly. Available at Tiong Bahru Market hawker stalls.

Where to buy: Tiong Bahru Market wet market section or any FairPrice / Cold Storage supermarket. Buy solid block or cylinder form rather than liquid syrup for travel.

To take home: Wrapped block, carry-on or checked. Keeps for months stored cool and dry.

Pandan (Pandan Leaf / Daun Pandan)
▾

A long green leaf used across Southeast Asian cooking as a natural flavouring and colouring — sometimes called the "vanilla of Southeast Asia." Produces a grassy, sweet, faintly nutty aroma. Used in rice, desserts, cakes, and drinks. Fresh leaves aren't worth taking home but extract and paste travel well.

What to try: Pandan layer cake (kueh pandan) at any good bakery — Tiong Bahru Bakery or a hawker centre kueh stall. Pandan coconut rice (nasi lemak) shows how it works in savoury cooking.

Where to buy: Pandan extract or paste at FairPrice or Cold Storage supermarket. Look for Koepoe Koepoe brand — widely available, reliable quality.

To take home: Small bottle of extract, carry-on liquid rules apply (under 100ml or checked). Paste in sealed jar is checked luggage.
