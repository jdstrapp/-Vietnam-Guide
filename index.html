Avoid touts inside the terminal. #### Getting Around Town Most sites in District 1 are walkable. Districts 3 and 5 (Cholon) require Grab. #### Weather Daytime high: 33–34°C / 91°F Nighttime low: ~23°C Conditions: Hot, dry season UV index: Very high Note: February is one of the best months to visit. Carry a large scarf or sarong for temple visits — temples don't provide coverings. #### Washrooms More variable than elsewhere on this trip. In cities, cafés and restaurants are your most reliable option — step in and ask. Public toilets exist near markets and tourist sites and usually charge 2,000–5,000 VND; keep small change handy. Things to Eat and Buy #### Vietnamese coffee — what to drink & learn Vietnam is the world's second-largest coffee producer, built on robusta beans brewed through a phin filter. HCMC is the best city on the trip to learn about it in depth. Must-try styles: ca phe sua da (iced milk coffee with condensed milk), ca phe trung (egg coffee), ca phe dua (coconut coffee — best at Can Tho). To take home: a phin filter (~$5–10) and single-origin robusta from the Central Highlands. See Coffee section for cafes and the Lacaph workshop. #### Bean-to-bar chocolate Vietnam is the origin of Marou — the world-class bean-to-bar brand made entirely from Vietnamese cacao. HCMC is the best place on the trip to explore it. See Chocolate section below. #### Propaganda art prints Iconic Vietnamese socialist poster art — originals and reproductions widely available across District 1. #### Fish Sauce (Nước Mắm) Fermented anchovies and salt, aged 12–15 months in wooden barrels. The fundamental condiment of Vietnamese cooking — used raw in dipping sauces (nước chấm), cooked into braises, and drizzled over almost everything. Quality varies enormously by grade. Grades: * 30°N — everyday cooking grade * 40°N — premium; best for dipping where quality is most obvious * 43°N — top-end, longer shelf life, more complex * 60°N — rare first-press; unlikely outside Phu Quoc Tasting: Compare the dipping sauce (nước chấm) at different restaurants — ratios and quality vary noticeably and reflect the cook's hand. Good fish sauce smells savoury and almost caramel-like, not aggressively fishy. Factory tours: Phu Quoc island (not on itinerary) has 100+ factories open to visitors. Best is Khai Hoan — free English tours, no booking needed. Worth considering if Phu Quoc ever gets added as a side trip. Where to buy in HCMC: Bến Thành Market East Gate (Phan Boi Chau St) — packaged goods section, multiple brands side by side. Look for Red Boat or Phú Quốc brand, 40°N+. To take home: Sealed bottles, checked luggage only. Red Boat 40°N is also available in Canada but significantly cheaper here and fresher stock. #### Cashews (Hạt Điều) Vietnam is one of the world’s largest cashew producers, primarily from Binh Phuoc province near HCMC. Quality at source is noticeably better than anything exported. Look for whole kernels (not broken), ivory to light gold colour, fresh smell. W180 or W240 on packaging indicates large, high-grade kernels. Varieties to try: • Salt-roasted whole — the baseline, shows quality clearly • Salt and kaffir lime leaf — a southern Vietnamese twist, excellent • Coconut-roasted — common in the Mekong region Where to buy in HCMC: Bến Thành Market East Gate alongside fish sauce, but tourist-priced — negotiate. Better value at local dry goods shops in the same area. Avoid pre-packaged tins in tourist shops — usually stale. To take home: Vacuum-sealed packs travel well in checked or carry-on luggage. Far cheaper than Canada and noticeably fresher. #### Dried Shrimp (Tôm Khô) Small shrimp sun-dried to an intense, concentrated umami — one of the most useful pantry ingredients in Vietnamese and broader Southeast Asian cooking. Used in fried rice, congee, soups, salad dressings, and as a topping. A small handful transforms a dish. Mekong Delta produces some of the best in Vietnam. What to look for: Bright orange-pink colour, firm texture, clean sea smell. Avoid anything grey, powdery, or with ammonia smell. Sold by weight at markets — buy loose rather than pre-packaged where possible. Where to buy in HCMC: Bến Thành Market, Bình Tây Market (Cholon — larger, more local, better prices). To take home: Sealed bag, checked luggage. Lasts months in a cool dry place, longer refrigerated. #### Durian Products (Sầu Riêng) Off-season for a Feb–Mar visit — fresh Vietnamese durian runs May–September, peaking in June. Not worth seeking out. Best tried in Singapore instead. 🏷 Skip here — try in Singapore Fresh durian — the famously pungent, custard-textured fruit — is not transportable home but worth trying in HCMC or the Mekong Delta where it’s grown locally. February is slightly off-peak but available. Monthong variety is the mildest entry point; Musang King more intense. What to try fresh: Buy pre-opened from a market stall rather than whole — vendors will crack it and let you smell before committing. Eaten with sticky rice in some southern preparations. Packaged versions for travel: • Dried durian chips — concentrated flavour, addictive once you’re converted • Durian candy and wafers — milder, good introduction • Freeze-dried durian — closest to fresh texture, best quality option Where to buy in HCMC: Bến Thành Market or any street fruit stall for fresh. Lasts several weeks refrigerated. #### Gula Melaka (Palm Sugar) Dark, aromatic sugar made from the sap of coconut palm flowers — used across Southeast Asian cooking and baking. Tastes like a complex cross between brown sugar, molasses, and caramel with a faint smokiness. Sold in cylinders or discs. Completely different character from refined sugar and irreplaceable in dishes like cendol, ondeh ondeh, and coconut-based desserts. What to try: Order cendol — a shaved ice dessert with pandan jelly, coconut milk, and gula melaka syrup poured over. Simple but shows the sugar's character clearly. Available at Tiong Bahru Market hawker stalls. Where to buy: Tiong Bahru Market wet market section or any FairPrice / Cold Storage supermarket. Buy solid block or cylinder form rather than liquid syrup for travel. To take home: Wrapped block, carry-on or checked. Keeps for months stored cool and dry. #### Pandan (Pandan Leaf / Daun Pandan) A long green leaf used across Southeast Asian cooking as a natural flavouring and colouring — sometimes called the "vanilla of Southeast Asia." Produces a grassy, sweet, faintly nutty aroma. Used in rice, desserts, cakes, and drinks. Fresh leaves aren't worth taking home but extract and paste travel well. What to try: Pandan layer cake (kueh pandan) at any good bakery — Tiong Bahru Bakery or a hawker centre kueh stall. Pandan coconut rice (nasi lemak) shows how it works in savoury cooking. Where to buy: Pandan extract or paste at FairPrice or Cold Storage supermarket. Look for Koepoe Koepoe brand — widely available, reliable quality. To take home: Small bottle of extract, carry-on liquid rules apply (under 100ml or checked). Paste in sealed jar is checked luggage. #### Bean-to-bar chocolate Singapore has a strong homegrown scene worth exploring alongside the Vietnamese cacao story. See Chocolate in See & Do ### 3.
