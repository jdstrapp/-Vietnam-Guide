Taiwan is a small island of 25 million people off the southeast coast of mainland China, shaped by successive waves of indigenous, Han Chinese, colonial, and Japanese rule before becoming the seat of the Republic of China government in 1949. Taipei is its economic, political, and cultural centre: a cosmopolitan metropolis where incense-veiled temples blend seamlessly with neon modern street life.

The metro area holds around 4.5 million people, known for its world-class MRT, legendary night markets, and vast array of restaurants. Food is central to Taiwanese culture, costs are low, and ease of navigation frequently surprises first-time visitors.

Mid-February is a good time to arrive — Lunar New Year will have just passed and the island settles back into its everyday rhythm.

Pack a light jacket or thick hoodie, waterproof shell, and water-resistant shoes. Long pants. A humidity you feel in the bones — not bitter cold, just persistent dampness.

MRT — Clean, fast, affordable. Get an EasyCard on arrival — works on MRT, buses, and in convenience stores. Airport Express from TPE to Taipei Main Station: ~35 min, NT$160. citizenM is an 8-minute walk from Taipei Main Station.

Washrooms — Easy. Convenience stores (7-Eleven, FamilyMart, Hi-Life) almost always have clean, free washrooms — there are hundreds across the city. MRT stations are reliable too, and accessible before the fare gates so no ticket needed. Essentially a non-issue.

Ho Chi Minh City — still widely called Saigon — is Vietnam's largest city and its economic engine, with a population of over 9 million. Its history runs from a Khmer fishing village to a French colonial stronghold to the focal point of the Vietnam War, renamed after the fall of Saigon in 1975.

For travellers, it's a city of relentless energy — chaotic motorbike traffic, excellent coffee, war history you can walk through, and neighbourhood life that rewards anyone willing to get off the main drag.

Daytime high33–34°C / 91°F
Nighttime low~23°C
ConditionsHot, dry season
UV indexVery high
February is one of the best months to visit. Lightweight breathable clothes, SPF 30+ sunscreen, sun hat, sunglasses. Carry a large scarf or sarong for temple visits — temples don't provide coverings.

Grab — primary transport. Book from the app before exiting arrivals. Fare fixed upfront (~100,000–150,000 VND from SGN). Stick to Vinasun or Mai Linh metered taxis if Grab is surging. Avoid touts inside the terminal. Most sites in District 1 are walkable. Districts 3 and 5 (Cholon) require Grab.

More variable than elsewhere on this trip. In cities, cafés and restaurants are your most reliable option — step in and ask. Public toilets exist near markets and tourist sites and usually charge 2,000–5,000 VND; keep small change handy. Attended paid toilets are generally cleaner than free public ones.

In rural areas — including the Mekong Delta cycling route and Pù Luông — facilities are sparse. Use whatever's available before you leave a stop. Two rules that apply everywhere in Vietnam: carry your own toilet paper (rarely provided), and don't flush it — plumbing can't handle it. Use the bin beside the toilet.

Vietnamese coffee — what to drink & learn

Vietnam is the world's second-largest coffee producer, built on robusta beans brewed through a phin filter. HCMC is the best city on the trip to learn about it in depth. The Mekong is one of the great rivers of Asia, rising in the Tibetan Plateau and flowing nearly 4,500 km through China, Myanmar, Laos, Thailand, and Cambodia before reaching Vietnam. Its delta — where the river fans out into a vast network of channels, islands, and floodplains before emptying into the South China Sea — is one of the most fertile regions on earth, producing more than half of Vietnam's rice and the bulk of its fruit, fish, and vegetables.

Can Tho is the largest city in the Mekong Delta and its de facto capital. The food identity is strongly rooted in Southern Vietnamese riverside cuisine — fresher and sweeter than the north, built around abundant local seafood, vegetables, and rice.

The cycling route runs from the outskirts of HCMC through Ben Tre (Vietnam's coconut capital), Tra Vinh, and into Can Tho. No ATMs between stops — stock up on VND in HCMC before departing Feb 19.

Forget convenience stores — they're a city phenomenon in Vietnam and won't appear on rural Delta roads. What you'll find instead is better: a constant trickle of roadside stalls, market towns, and small family restaurants serving fresh, cheap food to order.

Stop when you see people eating. A busy stall with plastic stools and steam coming from a pot is always a good sign. No need to read a menu — point at what someone else is having.

Use market towns and ferry crossings as planned stops — gaps between settlements on back roads can be longer than expected.

Bánh mì

The easiest grab-and-go option. A vendor with a cart or basket, usually 15,000–30,000 VND. Portable, filling, no sit-down required.

Hủ tiếu — Delta noodle soup

The Delta's signature noodle soup. Light, clear broth with pork or shrimp. Fast to serve, deeply local, and cheap.

Cơm bình dân — workers' rice shops

Point at pre-cooked dishes in pots: grilled fish, stir-fried vegetables, rice. Usually 30,000–50,000 VND for a full plate.

Fresh fruit at source

Sold cut and bagged roadside for almost nothing. The Delta grows Vietnam's best: star apple, pomelo, citrus, coconut. Excellent mid-ride fuel.

Nước mía — sugarcane juice

Pressed on the spot, cold, about 10,000–15,000 VND. Hard to pass up in the heat.

Cai Rang Floating Market
Before 6am

Early morning boat trip — one of the Delta's most famous markets. Vendors on boats selling fresh produce, coconuts, and local goods. Go by 6am.

Hanoi is Vietnam's capital — a thousand-year-old city whose heartbeat emanates from the Old Quarter, where 19th-century teak-beamed shophouses shelter cafes with French names and traditional music mingles with the sound of millions of motorbikes.

Where HCMC buzzes with commercial energy and forward momentum, Hanoi feels older and more layered — lakes, pagodas, French colonial architecture, and a slower pace.

Late Feb15–20°C
Early Mar18–24°C
ConditionsCool, misty, humid

Pù Luông

DatesFeb 28 – Mar 2
From Hanoi165 km · ~3.5 hrs
FormatVillage-to-village trek
About Health Considerations

Pù Luông Nature Reserve is the recommended trekking destination near Hanoi — flat valley paths, Black Thai and Muong villages, quiet, with extras including bamboo rafting and waterwheels. 165 km from Hanoi.

February is dry season — ideal conditions for trekking. Mist in the mornings lifts to clear skies by mid-morning.
Leeches

Leeches are the most likely nuisance. They're harmless but startling. Peak season is the rainy months (May–October); February is significantly better. Humid, shaded jungle sections at elevation can stay wet year-round, so don't rule them out entirely.

Tuck pants into socks. Standard insect repellent doesn't reliably deter leeches — salt works better. Check yourself after every forest section.

Mosquitoes

Mosquitoes are present year-round but reduced in the dry season. Dengue is the main day-to-day risk — spread by Aedes mosquitoes that bite during daylight hours, especially dawn and late afternoon. No treatment exists, so prevention matters: 20%+ DEET repellent, long sleeves and pants, and a bed net if sleeping somewhere without screens.

Japanese Encephalitis (JE) vaccination is worth discussing with a travel medicine clinic before departure — the Mekong Delta homestays and Pù Luông both involve rural outdoor exposure, which is exactly the profile the CDC flags.

Ticks, Snakes & Rabies

Ticks — present in jungle undergrowth. Do a full body check after any off-trail walking.

Snakes — low-probability but real hazard in forested areas. Stick to trails, don't reach into undergrowth, watch where you step. Your Pù Luông guide will know the terrain.

Rabies — present in Vietnam. Don't pet stray dogs or feed monkeys near temples.

Pack: DEET repellent, toilet paper, a small supply of tissues, and trekking poles if you have them — useful on slippery rice terraces.

Singapore is a city-state of around 6 million people packed into just 718 km² at the southern tip of the Malay Peninsula. A British colony until 1965, it became one of Asia's wealthiest cities through sheer economic transformation. For travellers, it's an easy, frictionless city — English everywhere, excellent public transit, exceptional food at every price point, and a natural bookend to a Southeast Asia trip before flying home.

Temperature25–32°C
ConditionsHumid year-round

SeasonsNone — constant
The coldest temperature ever recorded in Singapore is 19.4°C. Locals consider anything below 22°C something of an event. Identical packing to HCMC — lightweight breathables, compact umbrella or packable rain jacket. A light layer for aggressive air conditioning indoors.

MRT — Clean, fast, cheap, and covers virtually everywhere you'll want to go. Get a stored-value card (EZ-Link or Singapore Tourist Pass) at the airport. Changi is directly on the MRT (East-West line) — ~30 min to the city centre. Grab is also widely available for anywhere not on the MRT.

A non-issue. Clean, well-maintained washrooms throughout MRT stations, hawker centres, malls, and parks. No small change needed, rarely a fee. Toilet paper is standard.
