Taipei

Feb 15–16NT$ (TWD) · 1 CAD ≈ NT$22.70Airport: TPE (Taoyuan)
About
▾

Taiwan is a small island of 25 million people off the southeast coast of mainland China, shaped by successive waves of indigenous, Han Chinese, colonial, and Japanese rule before becoming the seat of the Republic of China government in 1949. Taipei is its economic, political, and cultural centre: a cosmopolitan metropolis where incense-veiled temples blend seamlessly with neon modern street life.

The metro area holds around 4.5 million people, known for its world-class MRT, legendary night markets, and vast array of restaurants. Food is central to Taiwanese culture, costs are low, and ease of navigation frequently surprises first-time visitors.

Mid-February is a good time to arrive — Lunar New Year will have just passed and the island settles back into its everyday rhythm.

Ho Chi Minh City

Feb 17–18, Feb 25–27VND · 1 CAD ≈ 19,000 VNDAirport: SGN (Tan Son Nhat)
About
▾

Ho Chi Minh City — still widely called Saigon — is Vietnam's largest city and its economic engine, with a population of over 9 million. Its history runs from a Khmer fishing village to a French colonial stronghold to the focal point of the Vietnam War, renamed after the fall of Saigon in 1975.

For travellers, it's a city of relentless energy — chaotic motorbike traffic, excellent coffee, war history you can walk through, and neighbourhood life that rewards anyone willing to get off the main drag.

▾

Book from the Grab app before exiting arrivals. Fare fixed upfront (~100,000–150,000 VND from SGN). Stick to Vinasun or Mai Linh metered taxis if Grab is surging. Avoid touts inside the terminal.

▾

Weather
▾

Daytime high: 33–34°C / 91°F

Nighttime low: ~23°C

Conditions: Hot, dry season

UV index: Very high

February is one of the best months to visit. Lightweight breathable clothes, SPF 30+ sunscreen, sun hat, sunglasses. Carry a large scarf or sarong for temple visits — temples don't provide coverings.

Washrooms
▾

More variable than elsewhere on this trip. In cities, cafés and restaurants are your most reliable option — step in and ask. Public toilets exist near markets and tourist sites and usually charge 2,000–5,000 VND; keep small change handy. Attended paid toilets are generally cleaner than free public ones.

In rural areas — including the Mekong Delta cycling route and Pù Luông — facilities are sparse. Use whatever's available before you leave a stop. Don't wait.

Two rules everywhere in Vietnam: carry your own toilet paper (rarely provided), and don't flush it — use the bin beside the toilet.

Vietnamese coffee — what to drink & learn
▾

Vietnam is the world's second-largest coffee producer, built on robusta beans brewed through a phin filter. HCMC is the best city on the trip to learn about it in depth. Must-try styles: ca phe sua da (iced milk coffee with condensed milk), ca phe trung (egg coffee), ca phe dua (coconut coffee — best at Can Tho). To take home: a phin filter (~$5–10) and single-origin robusta from the Central Highlands.

Bean-to-bar chocolate

Mekong Delta

Feb 18–24Cash only in rural areasMr. Biker Saigon cycling tour
About
▾

The Mekong is one of the great rivers of Asia, rising in the Tibetan Plateau and flowing nearly 4,500 km through China, Myanmar, Laos, Thailand, and Cambodia before reaching Vietnam. Its delta — where the river fans out into a vast network of channels, islands, and floodplains before emptying into the South China Sea — is one of the most fertile regions on earth, producing more than half of Vietnam's rice and the bulk of its fruit, fish, and vegetables.

Can Tho is the largest city in the Mekong Delta and its de facto capital. The food identity is strongly rooted in Southern Vietnamese riverside cuisine — fresher and sweeter than the north, built around abundant local seafood, vegetables, and rice.

The cycling route runs from the outskirts of HCMC through Ben Tre (Vietnam's coconut capital), Tra Vinh, and into Can Tho. No ATMs between stops — stock up on VND in HCMC before departing Feb 18.

Lunch on the Road
▾

Forget convenience stores — they're a city phenomenon in Vietnam and won't appear on rural Delta roads. What you'll find instead is better: a constant trickle of roadside stalls, market towns, and small family restaurants serving fresh, cheap food to order.

Stop when you see people eating. A busy stall with plastic stools and steam coming from a pot is always a good sign. No need to read a menu — point at what someone else is having.

Use market towns and ferry crossings as planned stops — gaps between settlements on back roads can be longer than expected.

Bánh mì — the easiest grab-and-go option. A vendor with a cart or basket, usually 15,000–30,000 VND. Portable, filling, no sit-down required.

Hủ tiếu — the Delta's signature noodle soup. Light, clear broth with pork or shrimp. Fast to serve, deeply local, and cheap.

Cơm bình dân — workers' rice shops. Point at pre-cooked dishes in pots: grilled fish, stir-fried vegetables, rice. Usually 30,000–50,000 VND for a full plate.

Fresh fruit — sold cut and bagged roadside. The Delta grows Vietnam's best: star apple, pomelo, citrus, coconut. Excellent mid-ride fuel.

Nước mía (sugarcane juice) — pressed on the spot, cold, about 10,000–15,000 VND. Hard to pass up in the heat.

Ca phe dua (coconut coffee)
▾

Best version on the entire trip. Floating market vendors at Cai Rang serve it with coconuts harvested that morning — rich, tropical, sweet. Order it wherever you stop along the river.

Best on the trip

Feb 25–28, Mar 5VND · 1 CAD ≈ 19,000 VNDAirport: HAN (Noi Bai)
About
▾

Hanoi is Vietnam's capital — a thousand-year-old city whose heartbeat emanates from the Old Quarter, where 19th-century teak-beamed shophouses shelter cafes with French names and traditional music mingles with the sound of millions of motorbikes.

Where HCMC buzzes with commercial energy and forward momentum, Hanoi feels older and more layered — lakes, pagodas, French colonial architecture, and a slower pace.

Pù Luông

Feb 28 – Mar 4From Hanoi: 165 km, ~3.5 hrsVillage-to-village trek
About
▾

Pù Luông Nature Reserve is the recommended trekking destination near Hanoi — flat valley paths, Black Thai and Muong villages, quiet, with extras including bamboo rafting and waterwheels. 165 km from Hanoi.

February is dry season — ideal conditions for trekking. Mist in the mornings lifts to clear skies by mid-morning.
Hotel
▾

Homestay

Getting To and From the Airport
▾

Transfer from Hanoi by private car or minibus, ~3.5 hrs each way.

Getting Around Town
▾

On foot — village-to-village trail format. Your guide will know the terrain.

Weather
▾

Cool, dry. February is dry season — ideal trekking conditions. Mornings misty, clearing by mid-morning.

Washrooms
▾

Sparse. Use what's available at homestays and rest stops. Carry toilet paper at all times.

Homestay meals
▾

Chicken, river fish, rice, vegetables — naturally accommodating for no red meat. Food is prepared by the homestay host family using local ingredients. Expect simple, fresh, honest cooking.

Village-to-village trekking
Top Pick
▾

Flat valley paths connecting Black Thai and Muong villages. Bamboo rafting, rice terraces, traditional waterwheels. The quietest, most authentic trekking experience accessible from Hanoi.

Health Notes
▾

Leeches: The most likely nuisance. Harmless but startling. Peak season is rainy months (May–Oct); February is significantly better. Humid jungle sections can stay wet year-round — tuck pants into socks. Salt works better than insect repellent. Check yourself after every forest section.

Mosquitoes: Reduced in dry season but present. Dengue is the main day-to-day risk — use 20%+ DEET, long sleeves and pants, and a bed net if sleeping without screens. Discuss Japanese Encephalitis vaccination with a travel clinic before departure.

Ticks: Present in jungle undergrowth. Full body check after any off-trail walking. Snakes: low-probability but real hazard — stick to trails, don't reach into undergrowth.

Rabies: Present in Vietnam. Don't pet stray dogs or feed monkeys near temples.

Pack: DEET repellent · toilet paper · tissues · trekking poles (useful on slippery rice terraces)

Black Thai & Muong Villages
▾

The valley path connects several traditional villages whose communities maintain distinct cultural practices, textiles, and architecture. The Vietnam Museum of Ethnology in Hanoi (see Hanoi section) is excellent context before the trek.

Homestay dining
▾

All meals at homestays along the route. No separate restaurant research needed — this is part of the experience.

Singapore

Mar 4–7 (depart Mar 9)SGD · 1 CAD ≈ S$0.93Airport: SIN (Changi)Base: Tiong Bahru
About
▾

Singapore is a city-state of around 6 million people packed into just 718 km² at the southern tip of the Malay Peninsula. A British colony until 1965, it became one of Asia's wealthiest cities through sheer economic transformation. English everywhere, excellent public transit, exceptional food at every price point, and a natural bookend to a Southeast Asia trip before flying home.

Hotel

Getting Around Town
▾

MRT — Clean, fast, cheap, and covers virtually everywhere you'll want to go. Get a stored-value card (EZ-Link or Singapore Tourist Pass) at the airport.

Weather
▾

Temperature: 25–32°C

Conditions: Humid year-round

Rain: Short, frequent

The coldest temperature ever recorded in Singapore is 19.4°C. Locals consider anything below 22°C something of an event. Lightweight breathables, compact umbrella or packable rain jacket. A light layer for aggressive air conditioning indoors.

Washrooms

A non-issue. Clean, well-maintained washrooms throughout MRT stations, hawker centres, malls, and parks. No small change needed, rarely a fee. Toilet paper is standard.

Durian

Varieties: Mao Shan Wang / Musang King (most prized), D24 (milder), Red Prawn (sweetest). Ask for free samples before buying. Banned on the MRT and in most hotel rooms — eat at the stall.
