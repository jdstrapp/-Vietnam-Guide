Taipei

Feb 14–16NT$ (TWD) · 1 CAD ≈ NT$22.70Airport: TPE (Taoyuan)citizenM North Gate
Mid-February is a good time to arrive — Lunar New Year will have just passed and the island settles back into its everyday rhythm.

Taiwan is a small island of 25 million people off the southeast coast of mainland China. Taipei is its economic, political, and cultural centre: a cosmopolitan metropolis where incense-veiled temples blend seamlessly with neon modern street life. The metro area holds around 4.5 million people, known for its world-class MRT, legendary night markets, and vast array of restaurants. Food is central to Taiwanese culture, costs are low, and ease of navigation frequently surprises first-time visitors.


Weather: Daytime high ~19°C / 66°F · Nighttime low 13–14°C · Cool, damp, grey, often drizzle. Pack a light jacket or thick hoodie, waterproof shell, water-resistant shoes.


Getting Around: MRT — Clean, fast, affordable. Get an EasyCard on arrival — works on MRT, buses, and in convenience stores. Airport Express from TPE to Taipei Main Station: ~35 min, NT$160. citizenM is an 8-minute walk from Taipei Main Station.


Washrooms: Easy. 7-Eleven, FamilyMart, Hi-Life convenience stores almost always have clean, free washrooms. MRT stations are reliable too. Essentially a non-issue.

Ho Chi Minh City

Feb 17–18VND · 1 CAD ≈ 19,000 VNDAirport: SGN (Tan Son Nhat)

Ho Chi Minh City — still widely called Saigon — is Vietnam's largest city and its economic engine, with a population of over 9 million. Its history runs from a Khmer fishing village to a French colonial stronghold to the focal point of the Vietnam War, renamed after the fall of Saigon in 1975. For travellers, it's a city of relentless energy — chaotic motorbike traffic, excellent coffee, war history you can walk through, and neighbourhood life that rewards anyone willing to get off the main drag.


Weather: Daytime high 33–34°C / 91°F · Nighttime ~23°C · Hot, dry season. Lightweight breathable clothes, SPF 30+ sunscreen, sun hat, sunglasses. Carry a large scarf or sarong for temple visits.


Getting Around: Grab — primary transport. Book from the app before exiting arrivals. Fare fixed upfront (~100,000–150,000 VND from SGN). Stick to Vinasun or Mai Linh metered taxis if Grab is surging. Most sites in District 1 are walkable.


Washrooms: More variable. In cities, cafés and restaurants are your most reliable option. Public toilets near markets usually charge 2,000–5,000 VND — keep small change. Two rules everywhere in Vietnam: carry your own toilet paper (rarely provided), and don't flush it — use the bin beside the toilet.

Mekong Delta

Feb 19–24Cash only in rural areas250km cycling · Mr. Biker Saigon

The Mekong is one of the great rivers of Asia, rising in the Tibetan Plateau and flowing nearly 4,500 km through China, Myanmar, Laos, Thailand, and Cambodia before reaching Vietnam. Its delta — where the river fans out into a vast network of channels, islands, and floodplains — is one of the most fertile regions on earth, producing more than half of Vietnam's rice and the bulk of its fruit, fish, and vegetables.


Can Tho is the largest city in the Mekong Delta and its de facto capital. The food identity is strongly rooted in Southern Vietnamese riverside cuisine — fresher and sweeter than the north, built around abundant local seafood, vegetables, and rice.


The cycling route runs HCMC → Cho Gao → Ben Tre (Vietnam's coconut capital) → Trà Vinh → Cầu Kè → Cần Thơ. No ATMs between stops — stock up on VND in HCMC before departing Feb 19.

Forget convenience stores — they're a city phenomenon in Vietnam and won't appear on rural Delta roads. What you'll find instead is better.

Stop when you see people eating. A busy stall with plastic stools and steam coming from a pot is always a good sign. Use market towns and ferry crossings as planned stops — gaps between settlements on back roads can be longer than expected.

Hanoi

Feb 25–27, Mar 3 (+ Mar 5 overnight)VND · 1 CAD ≈ 19,000 VNDAirport: HAN (Noi Bai)

Hanoi is Vietnam's capital — a thousand-year-old city whose heartbeat emanates from the Old Quarter, where 19th-century teak-beamed shophouses shelter cafes with French names and traditional music mingles with the sound of millions of motorbikes. Where HCMC buzzes with commercial energy and forward momentum, Hanoi feels older and more layered — lakes, pagodas, French colonial architecture, and a slower pace.


Weather: Late Feb: 15–20°C · Early Mar: 18–24°C · Cool, misty, humid. Mornings chilly before clearing by late morning. Light jacket for evenings. Raincoat, hat, sunglasses, and sunscreen for March. Comfortable walking shoes essential for cobbled Old Quarter streets.


Getting Around: Grab — primary transport. Walking is best within the Old Quarter. Airport: Noi Bai (HAN), ~45 km — allow 45–60 min by Grab.


Base: Tay Ho (West Lake) recommended — quieter, independent feel, lakeside. Old Quarter advised against as a base.

Pù Luông

Mar 1–5165 km from Hanoi · ~3.5 hrsVillage-to-village trekCash only — bring from Hanoi
February is dry season — ideal conditions for trekking. Mist in the mornings lifts to clear skies by mid-morning.

Pù Luông Nature Reserve is the recommended trekking destination near Hanoi — flat valley paths, Black Thai and Muong villages, quiet, with extras including bamboo rafting and waterwheels. The format is village-to-village: foot trails only, no motorbike-shared routes, with homestay accommodation connecting the communities.


Your criteria: 2–3 days, village-to-village, flat terrain, foot trails only. Pù Luông fits all of them — quieter and less crowded than Sapa, and directly relevant given the ethnic minority context you'll encounter.

Leeches
▾

The most likely nuisance. Harmless but startling. Peak season is the rainy months (May–October); February is significantly better. Humid, shaded jungle sections can stay wet year-round. Tuck pants into socks. Standard insect repellent doesn't reliably deter leeches — salt works better. Check yourself after every forest section.

Mosquitoes & Dengue
▾

Mosquitoes are present year-round but reduced in the dry season. Dengue is the main day-to-day risk — spread by Aedes mosquitoes that bite during daylight hours, especially dawn and late afternoon. No treatment exists, so prevention matters: 20%+ DEET repellent, long sleeves and pants, and a bed net if sleeping somewhere without screens. Japanese Encephalitis (JE) vaccination is worth discussing with a travel medicine clinic before departure.

Ticks, Snakes & Rabies
▾

Ticks are present in jungle undergrowth — do a full body check after any off-trail walking. Snakes are a low-probability but real hazard in forested areas; stick to trails, don't reach into undergrowth, watch where you step. Rabies is present in Vietnam — don't pet stray dogs or feed monkeys near temples.

Homestay meals
▾

Food is set by your hosts — no menus, no decisions. Expect river fish, free-range chicken, foraged greens, and rice at every meal. Simple, fresh, and genuinely excellent.

Cơm lam (bamboo tube rice)
Black Thai & Muong speciality
▾

Rice cooked inside a bamboo stalk over an open fire — smoky, fragrant, slightly sticky. Eaten by peeling back the bamboo. One of the most distinctive food experiences on the entire trip.

Rượu cần (communal rice wine)
▾

Communal rice wine drunk through long reed straws from a clay jar — a traditional part of village hospitality. You'll almost certainly be offered some.

Foraged vegetables and herbs
▾

Dishes will include whatever is growing locally — bamboo shoots, banana blossom, wild greens. This is the most authentic food on the trip.

Singapore

Mar 6–9SGD · 1 CAD ≈ S$0.93Airport: SIN (Changi)Base: Tiong Bahru

Singapore is a city-state of around 6 million people packed into just 718 km² at the southern tip of the Malay Peninsula. A British colony until 1965, it became one of Asia's wealthiest cities through sheer economic transformation. For travellers, it's an easy, frictionless city — English everywhere, excellent public transit, exceptional food at every price point, and a natural bookend to a Southeast Asia trip before flying home.


Weather: 25–32°C · Humid year-round · Short, frequent rain. Identical packing to HCMC — lightweight breathables, compact umbrella or packable rain jacket. A light layer for aggressive air conditioning indoors.


Getting Around: MRT — Clean, fast, cheap, and covers virtually everywhere you'll want to go. Get a stored-value card (EZ-Link or Singapore Tourist Pass) at the airport. Changi is directly on the MRT (East-West line) — ~30 min to the city centre. Grab is also widely available.


Washrooms: A non-issue. Clean, well-maintained washrooms throughout MRT stations, hawker centres, malls, and parks. No small change needed, rarely a fee. Toilet paper is standard.
